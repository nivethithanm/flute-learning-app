01 · Flute basics

Holding breath, holding bamboo.

Before notes, the body. A flute rewards a relaxed posture, a steady breath, and a soft, aimed stream of air across the blowing hole.

Parts of the flute

Blowing hole
The hole near the closed (cork) end you blow across — not into.
Finger holes
6 on a typical bansuri, often 7–8 on a Carnatic venu.
Closed end
Stopped just past the blowing hole; sets the air column's start.
Open end
The far end the air column "ends" at when all holes are shut.

The first sound

  • Rest the blowing hole on your lower lip, hole facing up.
  • Make a small "pooh" shape with your lips; aim a thin stream of air across the far edge of the hole.
  • Roll the flute slightly in/out to find the angle where the tone "locks in."
  • Start with all finger holes open — chase a clear tone before chasing notes.

Carnatic venu vs. Hindustani bansuri

 Carnatic (Venu / Pullanguzhal)Hindustani (Bansuri)
Typical holes7–8 finger holes6 finger holes (sometimes 7)
FeelFaster, ornament-dense gamakasLong breath, meend (glides)
Reference noteSa fixed by the chosen venuSa set by the bansuri's key
Note namesSa Ri Ga Ma Pa Da NiSa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni

The instrument is the same family — the difference is mostly in repertoire, ornamentation, and how Sa is fixed.

Breath

Breathe from the belly, not the chest. A note ends where breath ends — plan phrases so you're not gasping mid-line.

Posture

Shoulders down, neck long, flute roughly horizontal or angled slightly down. Fingers curved, pads (not tips) sealing the holes.

Choosing your first

A medium bansuri in G or A is a common, comfortable start. Smaller flutes (E, F) are easy to cover but shrill; long ones strain new hands.

A simple daily routine

  • Long tones (5 min) — hold each open-hole note steady; listen for a beat-free, even tone.
  • Sarali varisai (10 min) — the foundational ascending/descending patterns (see Songs & practice).
  • One raga's scale (5 min) — slow, in tune, then a little faster.
  • Play something you love (10 min) — use the tuner to check the notes you're unsure of.

02 · Sound & waves

A flute is a tuned column of moving air.

Blow across the hole and the air inside the tube vibrates as a standing wave. The length of that air column decides the pitch — and your fingers change that length.

—

Pitch, loudness, timbre

Pitch
How fast the air vibrates — its frequency in hertz (Hz). Faster = higher.
Loudness
How big the vibration is — its amplitude. Harder breath, louder note.
Timbre
The blend of harmonics riding on the fundamental — why a flute sounds like a flute.

Why the flute is an "open pipe"

Air can move freely at both the blowing hole and the open end, so both act as pressure "anti-nodes." The lowest standing wave that fits has a half-wavelength equal to the tube length:

f = v / (2L)

v ≈ 343 m/s (speed of sound in warm air). Halve the effective length and the pitch jumps an octave — exactly what overblowing does.

How each note "travels" down the tube

Opening a finger hole lets the air column end early there, shortening the effective length and raising the pitch. So the note isn't really moving — you're resizing the resonator. The table shows the idea for a flute whose Sa sits around 440 Hz.

SwaraFrequency (Hz)Effective lengthWavelength

Equal-tempered, A4 = 440 Hz. Real flutes use end-corrections and slightly different tunings, but the relationship — shorter column, higher pitch — always holds.

Harmonics & octaves

Blow a little harder and faster and the same fingering jumps to the 2nd harmonic — an octave up. This is how a flute reaches its upper register without extra holes. The 3rd harmonic is an octave-plus-a-fifth.

Cents: measuring "in tune"

An octave splits into 1200 cents; a semitone is 100. The tuner shows how many cents sharp or flat you are. Inside about ±5 cents sounds locked-in to most ears.

"Length" is relative — lower keys are longer flutes with a wider finger reach.

What changes when you change keys?

Nothing about the relationships between swaras — Sa to Pa is always a perfect fifth. Only the absolute pitches move. That's why notation written in swaras is portable across flutes: the same "S R G P D" plays correctly on a C flute and an A flute; it just sounds higher or lower.
